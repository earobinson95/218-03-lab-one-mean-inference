Question 10

If they say “90% chance”

  1. Careful! The true mean is either in our interval or not, but we don’t know. So, we can’t say we are 90% sure it is in there. It’s either 0% or 100%. We can’t estimate the chance that the parameter is in the interval. Instead, we are confident that if we make lots of intervals 90% of them should contain the population mean.

If they say “90% of the people”

  1. Our confidence interval is for the population mean, not the number of people! We are 90% confident the population mean hours worked per week for all working Americans is contained in our interval.

If they reference the sample mean instead of the population mean

  1. Careful! Our confidence interval is for the population mean, not the sample mean! We use our sample mean as the center of our confidence interval, so there is a 100% chance that it is capture in the interval! We don’t, however, know if our interval contains the true population mean.

If their interpretation doesn’t contain context

  1. “Which group of people are we interested in?”

  2. “Is our interval for the number of hours worked in a day? A week? A year?”

Question 11

If they don’t reject the null

  1. When making a hypothesis test decision with a confidence interval, we look for the null value in the interval. Is 40 contained in your 90% confidence interval?

If they talk about the interval being close to 40

  1. The 90% interval already captured the uncertainty for the sample mean, so now we need to see whether the null value overlaps with the interval. When making a decision, we’re not looking at how close or far 40 is from the interval.

Question 12

If they say “low probability of getting the null”

  1. The p-value isn’t the probability of getting the null, it is the probability of obtaining the results we saw or something more extreme is the null was true.

If they use 0.05 instead of 0.1

  1. Careful! Our significance level coincides with the confidence level we used. What percent confidence level did we use in the last problem? What significance level should you use?

General reminder about p-value interpretation

  1. The p-value represents the probability of obtaining the results we did (sample mean) or something more extreme, if the null was true. Given the p-value we obtained is 0.0376 (aka 3.76%), is it likely for us to have obtained a sample mean as large as what we saw due to chance alone?

General reminder about making a decision

  1. Remember when making a hypothesis test decision, we either reject the null or fail to reject the null!
